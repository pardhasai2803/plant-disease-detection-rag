DISEASE: Squash___Powdery_mildew
CROP: Squash (including zucchini, acorn squash, and other varieties of Cucurbita pepo)
TYPE: Fungal
SYMPTOMS: The primary symptoms of powdery mildew on squash include a white, powdery coating on the upper and lower surfaces of leaves, which can lead to yellowing, curling, and premature defoliation. Infected leaves may also develop brown or black spots, and in severe cases, the fungus can spread to the stems and fruit. As the disease progresses, the powdery coating can become more pronounced, and the leaves may become distorted or twisted.
CAUSES: Powdery mildew on squash is caused by the fungal pathogens Podosphaera xanthii and Erysiphe cichoracearum. These fungi overwinter on infected plant debris and can be spread by wind, water, and insects.
FAVORABLE_CONDITIONS: Powdery mildew on squash thrives in warm, humid environments with temperatures between 64°F and 90°F (18°C and 32°C). High relative humidity, particularly during the evening and early morning hours, and low air circulation can contribute to the development and spread of the disease.
CHEMICAL_TREATMENT: Chemical treatments for powdery mildew on squash include fungicides such as sulfur, copper-based products, and synthetic fungicides like triflumizole and myclobutanil. These products can be applied as foliar sprays or dusts, and their use should be rotated to minimize the development of fungicide-resistant strains of the fungus.
ORGANIC_TREATMENT: Organic treatments for powdery mildew on squash include neem oil, horticultural oil, and sulfur-based products. Additionally, spraying plants with a solution of 1 part milk to 10 parts water can help control the disease. Removing infected leaves and improving air circulation can also help to reduce the spread of the fungus.
PREVENTION: Preventing powdery mildew on squash involves a combination of cultural and sanitary practices. These include planting resistant varieties, providing good air circulation, avoiding overhead irrigation, and removing weeds and debris that can harbor the fungus. Regularly inspecting plants for signs of infection and removing infected leaves can also help to prevent the spread of the disease.
SEVERITY: High
ADDITIONAL_INFO: To manage powdery mildew on squash, it is essential to monitor weather forecasts and apply fungicides or organic treatments prior to periods of high humidity or rainfall. Removing infected plants and disposing of them in a sealed bag can help to prevent the spread of the disease to other parts of the garden. In addition, crop rotation and the use of physical barriers like row covers can help to reduce the incidence of powdery mildew on squash.
