DISEASE: Tomato___Leaf_Mold
CROP: Tomato
TYPE: Fungal
SYMPTOMS: Tomato Leaf Mold is characterized by the appearance of yellowish-green to yellow spots or patches on the upper surface of the leaves, which eventually turn brown and become necrotic. The underside of the leaves may exhibit a grayish-white to black moldy growth, especially in humid environments. Infected leaves may become distorted, curled, or droopy, and in severe cases, the disease can lead to defoliation and reduced fruit production.
CAUSES: Tomato Leaf Mold is caused by the fungal pathogen Passalora fulva (formerly known as Fulvia fulva or Cladosporium fulvum). The fungus overwinters on infected plant debris and can be spread through wind-borne spores, contaminated soil, and infected seedlings.
FAVORABLE_CONDITIONS: The disease thrives in humid environments with temperatures between 15°C to 25°C (59°F to 77°F). High relative humidity (above 80%), prolonged leaf wetness, and poor air circulation also favor the development and spread of the disease.
CHEMICAL_TREATMENT: Chemical treatments for Tomato Leaf Mold include the application of fungicides such as chlorothalonil, copper-based products, and strobilurins. These chemicals can be applied as foliar sprays or soil drenches, but it is essential to follow the recommended label rates and application intervals to avoid phytotoxicity and environmental contamination.
ORGANIC_TREATMENT: Organic treatments for Tomato Leaf Mold include the use of neem oil, sulfur-based products, and copper-based fungicides. Additionally, introducing beneficial microorganisms, such as Trichoderma harzianum, can help suppress the fungal pathogen. Organic growers can also use cultural practices like crop rotation, sanitation, and pruning to manage the disease.
PREVENTION: To prevent Tomato Leaf Mold, it is essential to maintain good hygiene practices, such as removing infected plant debris, disinfecting tools and equipment, and using disease-free seedlings. Other preventive measures include providing adequate air circulation, using row covers to reduce humidity, and avoiding excessive nitrogen fertilization, which can promote lush foliage and increase disease susceptibility.
SEVERITY: Medium to High
ADDITIONAL_INFO: Tomato Leaf Mold can be a significant problem in greenhouse and field-grown tomatoes, especially in regions with high humidity and cool temperatures. The disease can be managed through a combination of cultural, chemical, and organic practices. Regular monitoring and early detection are crucial to preventing the spread of the disease and minimizing yield losses. Growers should also consider using resistant or tolerant tomato varieties to reduce the risk of infection.
