DISEASE: Corn_(maize)___Northern_Leaf_Blight
CROP: Maize
TYPE: Fungal
SYMPTOMS: Northern Leaf Blight (NLB) is characterized by elongated, rectangular lesions that are typically 1-6 inches long and 0.5-2 inches wide. These lesions are usually grayish-green to tan in color, with a distinct border between the healthy and infected tissue. The lesions often appear in a linear pattern, following the veins of the leaf. As the disease progresses, the lesions can merge, causing large areas of the leaf to become blighted. In severe cases, the entire leaf can become necrotic, leading to reduced photosynthesis and decreased yields.
CAUSES: Northern Leaf Blight is caused by the fungal pathogen Exserohilum turcicum. This fungus overwinters in infected corn debris and can be spread through wind-borne spores, contaminated soil, and infected seed.
FAVORABLE_CONDITIONS: NLB thrives in cool, moist environments with temperatures between 64°F and 90°F (18°C and 32°C) and relative humidity above 80%. Prolonged periods of moisture, such as during rainy or irrigated conditions, can exacerbate the disease. Poor air circulation, high plant densities, and excessive nitrogen fertilization can also contribute to the development of NLB.
CHEMICAL_TREATMENT: Fungicides such as triazoles, benzimidazoles, and strobilurins can be effective in managing NLB. Examples of chemical treatments include azoxystrobin, pyraclostrobin, and propiconazole. These fungicides can be applied at the early stages of disease development, typically when lesions are first visible.
ORGANIC_TREATMENT: Organic treatments for NLB include copper-based fungicides, neem oil, and biological control agents such as Trichoderma harzianum. Crop rotation, sanitation, and the use of resistant cultivars can also help manage the disease. Organic farmers can also use cultural practices such as adjusting planting dates, using wider row spacings, and avoiding excessive nitrogen fertilization to reduce the risk of NLB.
PREVENTION: Preventative measures for NLB include using high-quality, disease-free seed, rotating crops to non-host species, and removing infected debris from the field. Planting resistant cultivars, maintaining good soil health, and avoiding excessive moisture can also help reduce the risk of disease. Regular scouting and monitoring for disease symptoms can help identify NLB early, allowing for timely and effective management.
SEVERITY: High
ADDITIONAL_INFO: Northern Leaf Blight can cause significant yield losses, particularly if left unmanaged. The disease can reduce grain yield by up to 30% and can also impact the quality of the grain. Resistant cultivars and integrated management strategies that combine cultural, chemical, and biological controls can help minimize the impact of NLB. Farmers should work with agricultural experts to develop a comprehensive management plan tailored to their specific production system and environmental conditions.
